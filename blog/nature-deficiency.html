Nature Deficit Society

As most people live in cities these days, many of us aren't exposed to nature — green, dense forests, mountains, starry nights, and beautiful landscapes. Rivers, waterfalls... it’s all becoming a distant dream. As people lose touch with it, they start craving it.

Businesses are capitalizing on this craving. Nature, which should be our right as humans, is now a product. Just look at Instagram — going into nature, seeing mountains and the earth in its pure form, has become a trend, a glamorous show-off spectacle. Young people see these nature reels and feel a sudden urge to go on a trip... and that’s it.

Tourism companies are making a business out of it. Was it always like this? I don't think so. Before we started destroying nature, people lived in it. It wasn’t sexy. It was harsh. It was normal. An ancient man wasn’t excited to see mountains — it was just there, part of life. They lived surrounded by nature and appreciated it constantly.

But not anymore. Today, you have to pay to see what we've been destroying for centuries. Think of any place with even a hint of beauty — you can’t just walk there. It’ll cost you. Locals have turned homes into homestays, and they monetize what they already have. And why wouldn’t they?

Some places get more tourists just due to geography. For example, in India, Manali is easily accessible and is flooded with visitors. Now, if you have even basic data analysis skills, you can see it clearly — more visitors means more content about it, more talk, and that creates a false perception that Manali is somehow more beautiful than other places. But beauty is highly subjective. You can’t rank nature.

People might like a place for a hundred different reasons — local experiences, culture, weather, personal mood. The whole marketing machine behind tourist destinations is one of the most degenerate things in society today. I've been to parts of Northeast India that are far more beautiful than Manali — but no one talks about them because they’re hard to reach, and the package-tour crowd avoids them.

We don’t even explore what the land we’re born in has to offer. Even on our so-called trips, we just waste time taking photos and creating video content. After building city lives and chasing economic dreams at the cost of nature, now we claim to care. Suddenly, we love nature... because we lack it. Yes, we are nature-deficit.

We don’t know how to live in nature, how to weave a basket from tree bark, how to survive in the wild. It’s not our lifestyle anymore. We don't even teach it in formal education. Of course not — formal education exists to train us to produce wealth for a few, essentially turning us into economic slaves.

So, we end up scrolling Instagram at midnight, staring at flashy videos of nature. That’s our relationship with the earth.

I was sick of these nature-obsessed people. So, I went to the mountains. I lived in Arunachal Pradesh — Tuting. I spent a long time there. Eventually, it started to feel normal, and I loved that feeling. Recently, while traveling to Tawang, I realized something — I had spent so much time surrounded by trees, rivers, and mountains that none of it caught my eye anymore. It never did. Even earlier.

I cringe when I see tourists in Arunachal losing their minds at something we are all part of — nature. What felt refreshing was seeing kids and locals in Tuting, who are surrounded by mountains daily. To them, it's just life. And they know how to live it.
