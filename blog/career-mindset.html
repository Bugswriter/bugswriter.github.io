Career : A Mindset of Slaves

Where Did the Idea of a "Career" Come From?

Many students today live under pressure, worrying constantly about their "career." But rarely do we stop to ask: Where did this concept originate? Why do teachers and schools keep asking kids, What do you want to become when you grow up? And why does this idea not work for so many people?

The idea of a "career" gained popularity largely due to the work of economist Adam Smith and his concept of the division of labor. Division of labor means breaking down work into smaller, specialized tasks so that each person performs one specific function, thereby increasing overall efficiency and output.

This model became central after the industrial revolution. As corporations rose, people's needs evolved, living standards shifted—and so did expectations. Jobs became the new currency of stability. Education turned into a filter for employment, and expertise became the system’s favorite metric. Companies started seeking individuals willing to dedicate their entire lives to just one narrow field.

Alienation in the Age of Specialization

As Karl Marx pointed out, this system produces alienation. Yes, people are "free" to choose their path, but that freedom is often illusionary. With rising expenses, societal norms, and an economy built on consumption, most people don’t feel free—they feel trapped.

Over time, the idea of a "career" restricts our thinking. We start believing we can only do one thing. We internalize competition. We silence our curiosity. And all of this pressure starts early—in school or college—when we’re still figuring out who we are.

Humans Are Not Machines

In ancient times, people weren’t bound to a single job for life. They explored multiple skills, sometimes out of necessity, sometimes out of passion. If someone stuck to one craft, it was never because a company demanded it—it was because they chose to.

Contrast that with the modern structure. Take YouTube as an example. If a channel posts consistently on one topic, it grows. But if that same creator experiments across different topics, the algorithm punishes them.

I’m an engineer who uses Linux. The entire Unix philosophy, which inspired Linux, is built on the idea that each program should do one thing and do it well. It’s a brilliant design principle—for software. But humans aren’t software.

Forcing people to specialize—to become a cog in one department—destroys their sense of novelty and joy. It may boost productivity, but it doesn’t nourish the soul. Doing many things imperfectly is far more human than doing one thing flawlessly under pressure.

The Invisible Hand of Platforms

YouTube’s algorithm isn’t designed for human freedom. It’s built for predictable growth. Channels with diverse content confuse the system, so they’re hidden. But what if a person has different interests? What if they treat their channel like a digital diary, not a business?

The platform doesn’t care. Diversity in content is punished, even if it's a reflection of human creativity. The same principle applies across modern life—uniformity is rewarded, diversity is sidelined.

Specialization as Survival

In the past, people grew their own food and had multiple domestic skills. Now, most of us outsource everything. But to outsource, we need money. And to earn money, we specialize.

Over time, we’re taught to believe that other areas of life are distractions. That a real "professional" gives 100% to their career. But this belief is not just toxic—it’s extreme.

Yes, productivity rises. The economy grows. But ask yourself: Is your life worth less than your country’s GDP?

This system creates intense competition, fuels mental illness, and removes freedom. Kids are brainwashed early into thinking their worth depends on specialization. That their identity must come from their job.

Career as Identity: A Modern Prison

Many people today can’t imagine a life outside their career. Their profession becomes their identity. It’s a subtle form of programming—where people, unknowingly, accept slavery-like ideas for the sake of structure, security, or approval.

The only escape is to mentally break free from the idea of a “career.” Do what you enjoy. Do what matters personally or culturally. You don’t need to dedicate your entire life to a single field. That’s not nobility—it’s extremism.

It’s okay to fail in the rat race. In fact, stepping away from it might be the only way to stay human.
